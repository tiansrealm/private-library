## Core Thesis

Running out of things to say is the #1 conversation killer. This guide organizes topics into 10 categories, each expandable into dozens of subtopics — ensuring you never hit a dead end.

## The 10 Topic Categories

| # | Category | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Understanding Each Other** | "What's the most attractive thing about me?" / "What would you change about me?" / Goals for next 12 months |
| 2 | **Interests & Hobbies** | Favorite sports, foods, movies, music, clothing styles, zodiac signs, games |
| 3 | **Romance & Intimacy** | Ideal date, views on cheating, who leads in a relationship, defining love |
| 4 | **Work & Daily Life** | Work environment preferences, boss dynamics, after-work routines, cooking |
| 5 | **Marriage & Family** | Ideal wedding, views on children, monogamy, personal space in marriage |
| 6 | **Supporting Parents** | How often she visits home, parents' relationship, plans for elderly care |
| 7 | **Life Ideals** | Dreams, aspirations, bucket list items |
| 8 | **Hypotheticals** | "If you could..." scenarios |
| 9 | **Emotional / Deep** | Fears, regrets, defining moments |
| 10 | **Playful / Fun** | Would-you-rather, embarrassing stories, childhood memories |

## How to Use

- Start with lighter categories (2, 4, 10) for early conversations
- Progress to deeper categories (1, 3, 5, 9) as comfort builds
- Use as a mental reference — don't run through them like a checklist
- Combine with topic expansion methods (parallel, upward, downward, forward association)

## Actionable Takeaway

Keep this as a mental index. When conversation stalls, mentally scan the categories for a natural pivot. The goal is never to interrogate — pick a category, share something of your own first, then invite her perspective.
