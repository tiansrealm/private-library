# Summary: How to Improve Your EQ

**Source files:** `improve-eq-zh.pdf` · `improve-eq-en.docx`

---

## Core Thesis

EQ is not innate — it's cultivated through experience. It boils down to three dimensions: (1) perceiving others' emotions, (2) controlling your own emotions, (3) aligning behavior with goals.

## The Three Dimensions

### 1. Perceive Others' Emotions

- **Read facial expressions** — 70% of emotion is shown on the face. If someone looks uncomfortable mid-conversation, reflect: did I say something wrong? Adjust.
- **Pay attention to responses** — short closing phrases ("Nice," "Good," "I need to eat") signal she's ending the topic. Don't "self-entertain" by monologuing.
- **Sense group atmosphere** — know what to say and what NOT to say. In relationships, when she says "I'm not upset," she may be accumulating unresolved frustrations you didn't notice.

### 2. Control Your Own Emotions

Emotional maturity stages:
1. Anger → Explosion → Regret
2. Anger → Suppression → Relief
3. Anger → Calmness → Peaceful detachment

- We know exactly which words hurt loved ones most — EQ means restraining yourself from saying them
- Replace "No, that's wrong" with "I see your point, but from another perspective…"
- If she cries over something small, never say "Is that worth crying over?" — comfort, don't prove logic

### 3. Align Behavior with Goals (Value Maximization)

- If your girlfriend wants to talk but you need sleep: "I have to wake up early, but I've missed talking to you. Let's chat a little." → She'll often let you rest. **Women care about attitude more than actions.**
- If she wants apples and you give bananas, your effort doesn't matter — understand what she actually needs
- "Being nice" ≠ "she will date me" — that's behavior contradicting your goal

## What EQ Is NOT

- Not suppressing emotions or pleasing everyone
- It IS: perceiving others, controlling yourself, responding appropriately, using emotion wisely for mutual benefit

## Actionable Takeaway

Practice the three dimensions deliberately: (1) Watch faces and responses during every conversation, (2) When angry, pause before reacting — aim for Stage 3, (3) Before acting, ask "does this action serve my actual goal?"
